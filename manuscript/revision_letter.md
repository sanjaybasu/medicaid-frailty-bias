# Revision Letter — Health Affairs Scholar Transfer Submission

**Manuscript title:** Redesigning Medicaid Frailty Algorithms: Improved Identification of Medically Frail Adults Under Community Engagement Requirements

**Authors:** Sanjay Basu, MD PhD; Seth A. Berkowitz, MD, MPH

**Original submission:** Health Affairs Ms. No. 2026-00331
**Transfer destination:** Health Affairs Scholar (HAS)
**Date of revision letter:** April 12, 2026

## Cover Statement

We thank the editors and reviewers for their careful reading of our manuscript and their detailed, constructive comments. We have substantially revised the manuscript and eAppendix to address all concerns. The most significant changes are: (1) a new step-by-step description of how the status quo base case is constructed and explicitly identified as a *simulated*, not observed, quantity (eAppendix A.5); (2) an empirical validation comparing simulated identification rates to state-reported KFF exemption data (eAppendix A.6); (3) a structured ACS-to-ICD-10 crosswalk table showing exactly how disability domains are operationalized (eTable A2); (4) a table of diagnostic conditions more likely to be missed by restrictive algorithms (eTable A3); (5) a pre-specified sensitivity analysis excluding differentially under-documented Z-codes (eAppendix B.6); (6) clarification of the T1019 data source as a geographic provider-density correlate used only in supplementary geographic analyses; (7) clarification that the ADL threshold change affects the Channel A numerator while the denominator (ACS-disabled adults) is held fixed; and (8) expanded policy recommendations with five specific elements of minimum algorithmic design standards.

Below we provide a verbatim record of all editor and reviewer comments alongside our detailed responses and the precise locations of all revisions in the manuscript and eAppendix.

### REVIEWER 1, SPECIFIC CONCERN 2 — T1019 CPT CODE ROLE

| Column 1: Verbatim Reviewer Comment | Column 2: How We Fixed It | Column 3: Location of Revision |
|---|---|---|
| "It was unclear what the role of provider intensity data was in the simulation or why a single CPT code for personal care services was used as a surrogate for provider billing intensity. Personal care services are commonly billed for Medicaid-funded HCBS, which a large proportion of Medicaid beneficiaries with frailty receive. However, these services are typically billed by direct care workers who provide in-home support services rather than clinicians (e.g., MDs, DOs, or NPs) who would likely be responsible for certifying a beneficiary's medical frailty. The choice of the CPT code and how it's used was not clarified." | The reviewer raises a crucial clarification we failed to provide. We have substantially revised the relevant text in both the main manuscript and eAppendix A.3 to clarify all three points the reviewer identifies. First, on *what T1019 is*: T1019 is a HCPCS Level II procedure code (not a CPT code; these are technically distinct but often colloquially conflated) for Personal Care Services—attendant-level in-home ADL assistance, typically billed by direct care workers and personal care agencies, not by physicians. We now explicitly state this in eAppendix A.3 with this distinction. Second, on *who bills T1019*: we now explicitly note that T1019 is billed by direct care workers rather than clinicians, and that physician density (HRSA AHRF PCPs per 100K) is the data source used to parameterize physician certification capacity (Channel C rural penalty), not T1019. Third, on *what T1019 is used for in this study*: T1019 data enter exclusively as an ecological proxy for personal care service density in the geographic analyses (eFigures 5–7 and eAppendix C.4), reflecting the hypothesis that states with higher personal care service availability have higher claims-visible documentation of ADL needs. T1019 does not enter the main frailty microsimulation algorithm for any state. This is now clearly stated in both the main text and eAppendix A.3. | **Manuscript:** Methods → Study Design paragraph; T1019 sentence revised to read "…used exclusively as an ecological correlate for geographic provider-density analyses (eFigures 5–7) and did not enter the main frailty microsimulation algorithm (eAppendix A.3)" **eAppendix:** Section A.3, new paragraph "Clarification on T1019 role in this analysis" (~350 words) explaining the T1019 vs. CPT distinction, direct care worker vs. clinician billing distinction, and exclusive use as geographic correlate |

### REVIEWER 1, SPECIFIC CONCERN 3 — ICD-10 OPERATIONALIZATION

| Column 1: Verbatim Reviewer Comment | Column 2: How We Fixed It | Column 3: Location of Revision |
|---|---|---|
| "How the authors operationalized the base and expanded diagnostic code lists to identify medical frailty in their simulation was not clear. As I understand it, this study doesn't use any claims data capturing diagnoses on the medical record. Instead, I believe the authors use a mapping of diagnostic codes to self-reported functional needs or social needs (in the case of Z-codes) and then make assumptions about the rate at which those would be coded in a claims database. But as the authors acknowledge, this is not 1:1 because many people do not receive care to address their needs, and providers may not comprehensively document certain types of codes (Z-codes, in particular). It was unclear to me how their model would assume various sets of functional or social needs would show up in claims, or if they considered that some codes (like Z-codes) might be differentially under-used (see, e.g., https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama-health-forum/fullarticle/2836780). This could substantially affect the ability of a more inclusive algorithm to capture people with social needs." | The reviewer is correct on all counts, and we had not sufficiently explained the ACS-to-ICD-10 mapping step or addressed differential Z-code under-documentation. We have added three new elements to address this. First, we added a structured ACS disability domain to ICD-10 diagnostic family crosswalk table (eTable A2) showing exactly which ACS domains (DEAR, DEYE, DREM, DPHY, DDRS, DOUT) map to which ICD-10 code ranges and under what mapping logic. Second, we expanded the Channel A description in the main text Methods to explicitly describe this crosswalk, including the key limitation that "individuals with qualifying self-reported disability who have never received a corresponding diagnosis would not be captured in claims under any algorithm design." Third, we added a pre-specified Z-code sensitivity analysis (eAppendix B.6) that reruns the redesigned algorithm excluding Z59 and Z60 from the expanded diagnostic list. This analysis directly tests the reviewer's concern that differential Z-code under-documentation could undermine the benefit of including these codes. Results show the redesigned algorithm retains substantial positive gains without Z-codes (mean sensitivity 44.8% vs. 45.6% with Z-codes, a difference of only 0.8 percentage points), confirming that the primary performance drivers are the non-Z-code expanded families and ADL threshold reduction. We cite Chatterjee et al. (2025, *JAMA Health Forum*)—the specific paper the reviewer references—as evidence for Z-code measurement bias (new reference 30 in the manuscript). | **Manuscript:** Methods → Channel A paragraph (substantially expanded with crosswalk description and Z-code caveat); Methods → Redesigned Algorithm item 1 (Z-code acknowledgment with sensitivity analysis reference and new citation 30) **eAppendix:** New eTable A2 (ACS-to-ICD-10 crosswalk, full table with implementation notes); New Section B.6 (Z-code sensitivity analysis, ~500 words + results table); New reference 30 (Chatterjee et al. 2025 *JAMA Health Forum*, doi:10.1001/jamahealthforum.2025.1923) |

### REVIEWER 1, SPECIFIC CONCERN 4 — ADL THRESHOLD AND NUMERATOR/DENOMINATOR

| Column 1: Verbatim Reviewer Comment | Column 2: How We Fixed It | Column 3: Location of Revision |
|---|---|---|
| "One of the policy dimensions the authors consider is lowering the ADL threshold for medical frailty determination. Would this not affect both the denominator (population eligible for medical frailty exemption) and the numerator (population identified)? It was unclear to me if the authors were considering both effects to the numerator and denominator when calculating the sensitivity rate, or if they were holding constant the population that met a sufficient level of medical frailty and then applying different algorithms to this population. It was difficult to follow this component of the simulation given that this moving part could affect the detection rate via changes in the numerator and denominator." | The reviewer correctly identifies a potential ambiguity in how the ADL threshold change interacts with the sensitivity calculation. We have added an explicit clarifying paragraph in the Redesigned Algorithm section. To be precise: (1) lowering the ADL threshold from 2 to 1 *expands* the pool of individuals deemed clinically eligible under Channel A—this is the Channel A input count, or the pre-detection numerator; (2) however, the denominator for all reported sensitivity rates is held *constant* throughout, defined as the total ACS-disabled adult population—individuals reporting difficulty in at least one of six ACS domains—under both status quo and redesigned algorithms; (3) therefore, sensitivity improvements reflect a larger Channel A input pool (more individuals pass the expanded ICD-10 + lowered ADL threshold gate) being subjected to the same stochastic detection and certification channels, against the same fixed denominator of ACS-disabled adults. This is now stated explicitly in the revised manuscript, including the key phrase: "sensitivity improvements reflect an expanded numerator (more individuals deemed clinically eligible) against a stable denominator." | **Manuscript:** Methods → Redesigned Algorithm, item 2 (ADL threshold), new paragraph added immediately after the item definition explaining numerator/denominator logic explicitly |

### REVIEWER 2, SPECIFIC CONCERN 3 — CMS MINIMUM STANDARDS: MORE DETAIL

| Column 1: Verbatim Reviewer Comment | Column 2: How We Fixed It | Column 3: Location of Revision |

| "'CMS should consider establishing minimum algorithmic design standards for state frailty determination systems under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.' in the discussion or policy implementation section, I'd like to see more unpacking of this suggestion--what should it look like?" | We have substantially expanded the Policy Implications section to provide five specific elements of what CMS minimum algorithmic design standards should include, grounded in the evidence from our simulation. The five elements are: (1) recognition of at least 10 ICD-10 diagnostic condition families (current range spans 5–13 across states; 10 would exclude only the most restrictive existing approaches); (2) mandatory health information exchange integration to supplement MMIS claims data; (3) full ex parte determination capability—automated screening without requiring individual application—to reduce procedural barriers; (4) claims adjudication lag under 3 months; and (5) elimination of physician certification requirements in favor of claims-based identification, consistent with current practice in six states.
